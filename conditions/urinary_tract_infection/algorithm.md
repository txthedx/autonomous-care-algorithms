# UTI: Bent 2002 decision rule for acute uncomplicated cystitis in women

A symptom-based pretest probability assessment derived from the Bent 2002 *JAMA* "Rational Clinical Examination" systematic review and aligned with IDSA/ESCMID 2010 empirical treatment guidance.

## Step 1 — Complicating factors check

If any of the following is present, the patient is outside the "uncomplicated cystitis in women" frame and the Bent rule does **not** apply. Broader workup (urine culture, imaging considerations, alternative diagnoses) is indicated.

| Factor | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Pregnancy | Different antibiotic and follow-up considerations; culture indicated. |
| Male sex | Anatomic differences and higher rate of complicated infection. |
| Uncontrolled diabetes or immunocompromise | Atypical organisms; higher complication rate. |
| Indwelling catheter or recent instrumentation | Different microbiology; biofilm. |
| Known urinary tract abnormality | Different microbiology and complication risk. |
| Recent antibiotic use | Higher resistance risk; culture indicated. |
| Symptoms more than seven days | Worry for pyelonephritis or alternative diagnosis. |
| Recurrent UTI (≥3 in 12 months or ≥2 in 6 months) | Different management framework. |
| Flank pain, fever, or systemic symptoms | Suggestive of pyelonephritis; different evaluation. |

Sources: Bent 2002 (commentary on excluded populations); Gupta 2011 (IDSA 2010); NICE NG109.

## Step 2 — Vaginal symptoms check

If vaginal discharge or vaginal irritation is present, UTI is less likely and alternative diagnoses (vaginitis, sexually transmitted infections) should be evaluated. Bent reported a negative likelihood ratio of approximately 0.3 for vaginal discharge and 0.2 for vaginal irritation.

A pelvic examination is typically recommended in this scenario.

Source: Bent 2002.

## Step 3 — Symptom count and probability band

Among women without complicating factors and without vaginal symptoms, count the number of cardinal UTI symptoms present:

- Dysuria
- Urinary frequency
- Hematuria
- Suprapubic or back pain

| Symptom count | Pretest probability | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Low | Consider alternative causes; UTI unlikely on history alone. |
| 1 | Intermediate (approximately 50%) | Urinalysis dipstick or urine culture; treat if positive. |
| 2 or more | High (approximately 90%) | Empirical treatment for uncomplicated cystitis reasonable per IDSA 2010, without requiring dipstick or culture in most settings. |

The 90% pretest probability band is most strongly supported when dysuria and frequency are both present in the absence of vaginal symptoms (Bent 2002).

## Important caveats

- Local antimicrobial resistance patterns drive empirical antibiotic choice; this module does not select agents.
- Persistent symptoms after empirical treatment require urine culture and reassessment.
- The Bent rule was derived in primary care populations; performance in emergency or specialty settings may differ.
